Medieval Period

Medieval art evolved from the creative heritage of the Roman Empire and the iconographic practices of the Early Christian church, as well as the "barbarian" style of Northern Europe. The combination of these elements resulted in the creation of a magnificent artistic heritage. The Medieval art period had a variety of artistic trends, the most prominent of which were the Early Christian art movement, the Byzantine art movement, the Romanesque art movement, and the Gothic art movement.


Religious symbolism and imagery were prevalent in Middle Ages paintings. Medieval artists and their paintings primarily depicted religious figures and biblical stories. These storylines had a hierarchy that was mostly determined by the places that the paintings would fill. More major scenes in Medieval artwork would occupy prominent focal points in a church or altarpiece, while less significant scenes would radiate outwards.

Christ Pantocrator (c. 500 – 600)

The artist is unknown, but the Christ Pantocrator, which translates to "Christ the Saviour," comes from the 6th century. The picture represents Christ with a raised right hand, which symbolized his gift of blessing, and a Gospel book studded with diamonds in the shape of a cross in his left arm.

The picture is purposefully asymmetrical to represent Christ's dualism. When glancing at the picture of Christ's face, the features on the side where Christ holds the Gospel (the right side of the artwork) are stern and rigid. It was painted with more depth and shadow, resulting in a more three-dimensional, realism-inspired portrayal of Christ. This realism represented Christ's human character. In contrast, Christ's face is depicted more serenely and quietly on the side where his right hand is lifted (the left side of the picture), and this represents Christ's divinity.

Lamentation (The Mourning of Christ) (1306)

Giotto's Lamentation (The Mourning of Christ) is part of the Scrovegni Chapel's fresco sequence depicting the Life of Christ and the Life of the Virgin. The artwork represents the scene immediately following Christ's crucifixion, when his lifeless corpse was attended to by haloed relatives and disciples. Mary, the focal point, caressed Christ's head as Mary Magdalene wept at His feet.

Giotto firmly broke away from the Byzantine style and pioneered the skills of drawing precisely from life and true to nature. It deviated from two-dimensional conventions by including depth and naturalized figures.

Maestà (c. 1308 – 1311)

Duccio di Buoninsegna, the founder of the Sienese School of Painting, was one of the finest Italian painters of the Middle Ages. The Maestà is often regarded as his masterpiece. The title Maestà meant The Virgin Mary in Majesty, and this artwork was part of a polyptych, therefore it was part of an altarpiece made up of numerous wooden panels.

The saints and angels in the Maestà are almost life-size. It is vital to note the Virgin Mary's relative size; she is the largest figure in the painting. This is due to Mary's primary duty of interceding between God and humans, since she was utilized by ordinary people to get access to God. The panels in the front represent the Madonna and Child enthroned, surrounded by saints and angels.

Annunciation with St. Margaret and St. Ansanus (1333)

Simone Martini and Lippo Memmi were Italian medieval painters who used the Gothic style in their work. The central panel of the painting represented the Annunciation, in which the archangel Gabriel, with an olive branch, knelt before the seated Virgin Mary and informed her that she would soon bear God's child. Gabriel is shown with an olive branch, which is a classic sign of peace. The vase between Gabriel and Mary on the floor represented purity.

Above it, in the central arch, was a group of angels with linked wings, where the Holy Spirit's dove had descended from heaven. The words "Hail, Mary, full of grace, the Lord is with thee" were etched in gold and extended from the angel's mouth to Mary.

Wilton Diptych (c. 1395 – 1399)

The panel showed the King kneeling in front of a view from heaven. England's patron saints, Edmund and Edward the Confessor, and John the Baptist, were presenting King Richard II to the Virgin Mary cradling the Christ Child, who was flanked by eleven winged angels. Richard II became King of England when he was eleven years old, hence the number eleven was significant.
The hands of the King were presented to deliver or receive Saint George's standard with the crimson cross. Christ's hand was lifted to bless the standard, as was King Richard II's rule, as if the Virgin Mary and Christ Child had granted the King the divine right to rule.

The Trinity (c. 1411 – 1427)

Rublev's picture depicted the angels who announced to Abraham and Sarah that they would have a son. More specifically, the three angels signified the Trinity, or the unity of God the Father, God the Son, and God the Holy Spirit. Although God is not depicted in The Trinity, the artwork can still demonstrate an ideal expression of God.
As the Trinity is unified, the three figures purposefully share identical traits. The concept here is that God is one, but in three people. The oneness of the Trinity was further highlighted by the blue clothes worn by each angel. The artwork shows that the three were engaged in a religious discussion by gesture and glance, and they surrounded the chalice, which was symbolic.

Presentation at the Temple (c. 1319-1348)

Ambrogio Lorenzetti's painting depicts Mary's presentation in the temple and purification forty days after giving birth, according to Hebrew traditions. Mary, identifiable by her blue cloak and scarlet attire, is holding out her son to Simeon and the prophetess Anna, both of whom recognize the Child as the Messiah. Anna is clutching a scroll that has a passage from the Gospels that describes this incident (Luke 22,38). The priest is sacrificing the presents brought by Mary and Joseph on the altar. On the far left, Joseph is portrayed alone.

Paradiesgärtlein: Little Garden of Paradise

The devotional image depicts Mary reading and the newborn Jesus playing the zither in a floral garden, together with three unnamed female and as many male saints. The tree of knowledge and the four rivers of paradise, however, are missing from this iconic portrayal of the biblical Garden of Eden. Rather, the image of the "Madonna in the Rose Hag" is blended here with symbols from the secular love garden (polygonal stone table with refreshments, refreshing fountain, passing the time by reading, making music, or playing, cherry as erotically understood fruits).
